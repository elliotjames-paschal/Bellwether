Does Liquidity Predict Accuracy?

Tight spreads and deep order books feel trustworthy. We tested whether they actually predict better forecasts.

B
Bell Team Bellwether Research
Data: 2,970 resolved markets from Polymarket and Kalshi (Oct 2025 – Feb 2026), plus 8 million time-series snapshots across 2,753 markets.

The Question

A market with a 1-cent spread and $50,000 of depth feels more refined than one with a 10-cent spread and $500 of depth. The tight spread suggests agreement; the deep book suggests conviction. But does any of this actually predict whether the price is more accurate? We tested this using orderbook data from Polymarket and Kalshi—both in cross-section (comparing markets to each other) and over time (tracking each market as it evolves toward resolution).

The Trap

The obvious approach is to correlate spread with Brier score. But there's a trap: if you use relative spread (spread divided by price), it's mechanically correlated with price level. Markets priced near 0% or 100% have extreme relative spreads by construction. Since prices near the extremes also have low Brier scores (predicting 95% and being right costs you almost nothing), you get a spurious correlation. The fix is simple: use absolute spread (best ask minus best bid, in cents). Absolute spread has almost no correlation with price (r = 0.04), so there's no confound to control for.

The Result

After confirming there's no confound, we tested whether absolute spread predicts accuracy. The answer is no. In cross-section, markets with tighter spreads are not systematically more accurate than markets with wider spreads. The correlation between spread and residual Brier score (actual minus expected) is 0.04—statistically significant with 3,000 markets, but explaining less than 0.2% of variance. Depth shows even less signal. Order book imbalance, which initially looked promising in exploratory analysis, also failed to survive proper controls.

The time-series analysis tells the same story. We trained a Random Forest on 6.4 million snapshots using every available feature: spread, depth, order book imbalance, price extremity, time in market life. The model achieved 55.6% R² on training data—but when tested on held-out markets (not just held-out snapshots), performance dropped to −20.9%. That's worse than predicting zero for everything. The model had memorized market-specific quirks, not learned any generalizable relationship between liquidity and accuracy.

Why, and What To Do

This null result has precedent. Tetlock (2008) found similar patterns in TradeSports, another limit order book exchange: liquidity doesn't improve calibration and actually worsens forecasting resolution. The mechanism is that naïve limit order traders subsidize informed flow without contributing information—more liquidity means more noise traders alongside informed ones, leaving the signal-to-noise ratio roughly constant. At the macro level, Clinton and Huang (2025) found that PredictIt—with an $850 position cap—outperformed the far more liquid Polymarket in forecasting the 2024 election, suggesting forecast quality depends on trader diversity, not capital depth. The practical implications are straightforward: don't discount a market just because it has wide spreads or shallow depth. Don't weight prices by liquidity when aggregating forecasts. The midpoint price already incorporates whatever information the market has. Observable orderbook features add nothing predictive beyond it.
