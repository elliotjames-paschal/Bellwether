Does Liquidity Predict Accuracy?

Tight spreads and deep order books feel trustworthy. We tested whether they actually predict better forecasts.

B
Bell Team Bellwether Research

A market with a 1-cent spread and $50,000 of depth feels more trustworthy than one with a 10-cent spread and $500 of depth. But does liquidity actually predict whether the price is more accurate?

We tested this using orderbook data from ~3,000 resolved markets on Polymarket and Kalshi. The answer is no. Markets with tighter spreads are not systematically more accurate than markets with wider spreads. Depth shows even less signal.

Prior Research

This null result has precedent. Tetlock (2008) found similar patterns in TradeSports: liquidity doesn't improve calibration and may actually worsen forecasting resolution. The mechanism is that naïve limit order traders subsidize informed flow without contributing information—more liquidity means more noise traders alongside informed ones, leaving the signal-to-noise ratio roughly constant.

At the macro level, Clinton and Huang (2025) found that PredictIt—with an $850 position cap—outperformed the far more liquid Polymarket in forecasting the 2024 election, suggesting forecast quality depends on trader diversity, not capital depth.

Implications

Don't discount a market just because it has wide spreads or shallow depth. Don't weight prices by liquidity when aggregating forecasts. The midpoint price already incorporates whatever information the market has. Observable orderbook features add nothing predictive beyond it.
